Achieving Sustainable Development Goal 6: Ensuring Access to Clean Water and Sanitation for All

Simplice A. Asongu†

†Centre for the Study of the Economies of Africa (CSEA), Abuja, Nigeria

Abstract:

Sustainable Development Goal 6 (SDG 6), as set by the United Nations, aims to ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all by the year 2030. This bold and ambitious goal addresses not only access to clean drinking water and basic sanitation facilities but also emphasizes the importance of improved water quality, wastewater treatment, and water resource management. The successful implementation of SDG 6 is fundamental for achieving broader sustainable development objectives, as access to clean water and sanitation is intrinsically linked to poverty eradication, public health, equity, and environmental protection. 

This article aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the challenges and opportunities in achieving SDG 6. It will undertake a detailed analysis of the key targets and indicators within the goal, emphasizing the importance of inclusive and participatory approaches. Furthermore, it will evaluate the progress made thus far by various countries and regions, utilizing a comparative analysis to identify best practices and areas that need further attention. In addition, this article will explore the financial implications of implementing SDG 6 and highlight the need for increased investment in water and sanitation infrastructure to meet the ambitious 2030 targets.

SDG 6 targets cover a wide range of issues, including universal access to safe and affordable drinking water, adequate sanitation and hygiene facilities, domestic and industrial wastewater treatment, water pollution reduction, integrated water resource management, and water-related ecosystems protection. Each target is accompanied by specific indicators that enable monitoring and measurement of progress. However, it is crucial to recognize that the challenges in achieving SDG 6 are diverse and intertwined, ranging from inadequate infrastructure and limited resources to governance issues, climate change, and population growth.

One of the key challenges is the pervasive lack of infrastructure for water and sanitation services, particularly in low-income countries. Many communities still rely on unsafe water sources, leading to increased vulnerability to waterborne diseases and environmental degradation. Improving access to clean water and sanitation requires significant investment in infrastructure, technology, and capacity building. Public-private partnerships can play a crucial role in mobilizing resources and expertise for infrastructure development, operation, and maintenance.

In addition to infrastructure, ensuring sustainable management of water resources is integral to achieving SDG 6. Climate change exacerbates the challenges already faced, leading to increased water scarcity, droughts, and natural disasters. Integrated water resource management, involving the equitable and efficient allocation of water resources, is essential to prevent conflicts and ensure long-term sustainability. This requires effective water governance, stakeholder participation, and the adoption of innovative approaches.

While progress has been made towards SDG 6, many countries and regions still face significant gaps in achieving the targets. For instance, Sub-Saharan Africa is the most off-track in terms of access to safe drinking water and improved sanitation facilities. Comparable to this scenario is the global south where pockets of poverty and inequalities prevail. To bridge these gaps, it is imperative to prioritize the needs and vulnerabilities of marginalized and disadvantaged communities. Policy interventions should be targeted towards addressing the root causes of water and sanitation challenges, such as poverty, social inequality, and inadequate governance. Information and communication technologies (ICTs) can also play a transformative role in data collection, monitoring, and targeted service delivery.

Meeting the financing needs for SDG 6 is of paramount importance. Estimates suggest that an annual investment of $114 billion would be needed to achieve universal access to basic water and sanitation services by 2030. Financing mechanisms such as blended finance, impact investing, and innovative public-private partnerships can help mobilize the required funds. Furthermore, it is essential to ensure that water and sanitation investments are prioritized in national budgets, supported by international development assistance and financing institutions.

In conclusion, SDG 6 represents a critical step towards achieving broader sustainable development objectives. The successful implementation of SDG 6 requires a multi-faceted approach, addressing various interrelated challenges and harnessing synergies between different targets and indicators. By investing in water and sanitation infrastructure, improving water quality, and adopting innovative approaches to water resource management, we can ensure universal access to clean water and sanitation for all by 2030. Achieving SDG 6 will not only improve health outcomes and reduce poverty but will also contribute to ecosystem protection, economic development, and social equity. It is therefore incumbent on governments, civil society, and the private sector to actively engage in collaborative efforts to accelerate progress towards this transformative goal.
