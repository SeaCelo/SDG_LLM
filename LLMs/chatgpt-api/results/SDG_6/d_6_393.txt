
In today's world, access to clean water and sanitation remains one of the most pressing challenges for many communities across the globe. The Sustainable Development Goal 6 (SDG 6) aims to ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all by 2030. As an expert in international development, the Adventist Development and Relief Agency (ADRA) is committed to providing insight and analysis on the current progress of SDG 6.

Access to clean water sources is essential for human development, health, and well-being. Yet, according to the World Health Organization (WHO) and UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme, approximately 2.2 billion people lack access to safe drinking water. This alarming figure is aggravated by disparities between urban and rural areas, and within different regions of the world. Therefore, achieving universal access to safe and affordable drinking water remains a significant challenge within the current SDG framework.

Improving access to basic sanitation facilities is another critical aspect of SDG 6. Despite notable progress, approximately 4.2 billion people still do not have access to adequate sanitation facilities. This lack of access poses severe health risks, as it enables the spread of waterborne diseases such as cholera and typhoid, particularly in vulnerable and marginalized communities. Moreover, the absence of proper sanitation facilities disproportionately affects women and girls who face additional risks and limitations.

To tackle these challenges, efforts to evolve and adapt sustainable technologies, infrastructure, and innovative financing mechanisms are crucial. Ensuring equitable access to drinking water and sanitation requires collaboration between governments, civil society organizations, private sectors, and local communities. This multi-stakeholder approach fosters cross-sector coordination, knowledge sharing, and the mobilization of resources needed to make sustainable progress.

Applying a human rights-based approach is also pivotal in achieving SDG 6. Recognizing the right to clean water and sanitation empowers individuals and communities to demand their rights and hold duty-bearers accountable. Moreover, this approach emphasizes the importance of leaving no one behind, targeting the most vulnerable populations and addressing existing gender, socioeconomic, and geographic disparities in access.

In terms of progress, the global community has made significant strides towards achieving SDG 6. However, challenges and inequalities persist, necessitating renewed effort and commitment. Several countries have achieved substantial success in expanding access to improved water sources and sanitation facilities, showcasing innovative approaches and best practices. For instance, Rwanda has achieved almost universal access to improved water sources through a combination of policies, infrastructure investments, and sustainable management schemes.

However, it is critical to recognize the interconnected nature of SDG 6 with other Sustainable Development Goals. For example, ensuring sustainable water management requires addressing climate change, soil conservation, and protecting fragile ecosystems. Hand in hand with SDG 13 (Climate Action), efforts to mitigate and adapt to climate change, particularly in vulnerable regions, will be vital to safeguarding water resources and ensuring their availability for future generations.

In conclusion, although progress has been made, the achievement of SDG 6 remains a complex endeavor, particularly in underserved regions and vulnerable communities. Stakeholders must continue to collaborate, increase investment, and implement innovative approaches to promote sustainable water and sanitation management. By targeting the root causes of inequality, adopting a human rights-based approach, and harnessing technical expertise and financial resources, we can realize the vision of universal access to clean water and sanitation by 2030. The Adventist Development and Relief Agency supports and advocates for the acceleration of actions towards SDG 6 to ensure a healthier and more sustainable future for all.
