It is our privilege as the Liechtensteinische Entwicklungsdienst to present an in-depth analysis of Sustainable Development Goal 6 (SDG 6), which focuses on ensuring the availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all. In this report, we aim to provide comprehensive insights into the current state of SDG 6, the existing challenges, and potential solutions to achieving its ambitious targets. Our target audience consists of experts who specialize in water management and sustainable development.

SDG 6 reflects the international community's recognition of the pressing need for clean and accessible water sources and proper sanitation facilities. It is aligned with other relevant UN goals, such as SDG 1 (No Poverty), SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being), SDG 13 (Climate Action), and SDG 14 (Life Below Water). Each element of this goal is fundamental for socio-economic development, public health, and the preservation of the environment.

While great strides have been made since the goal was adopted in 2015, we must acknowledge the persisting challenges that prevent us from achieving SDG 6 by the target year of 2030. The first challenge lies in the lack of access to clean water sources and sanitation services. Currently, about 2.2 billion people globally lack access to safely managed drinking water, and 4.2 billion people lack access to safely managed sanitation facilities. Moreover, many vulnerable communities, such as those in remote areas or conflict zones, face a particularly daunting task in attaining water and sanitation security.

The second challenge is water scarcity, which is exacerbated by climate change, population growth, and unsustainable consumption patterns. As demand for water increases, it is projected that nearly half of the world's population will be living in water-stressed areas by 2030. This puts significant strain on ecosystems, agriculture, and industries, jeopardizing long-term development prospects.

Widespread pollution and inadequate wastewater management constitute the third challenge. Industrial runoff, agricultural activities, and improper disposal of waste contaminate water sources, impair ecosystems, and endanger public health. Efforts to treat and recycle wastewater are urgently needed to alleviate the looming crisis and contribute to sustainable water management.

Addressing these challenges requires multi-stakeholder collaboration, enhanced financing, and transformative policies. Governments must prioritize SDG 6 in national planning processes, allocate appropriate resources, and develop comprehensive strategies tailored to local contexts. Collaboration between governments, civil society, and private sector entities is essential for catalyzing innovations, scaling up successful initiatives, and mobilizing necessary investments.

Furthermore, it is crucial to strengthen water governance and institutional frameworks by promoting integrated water resources management, capacity building, and effective regulation. Transparent and participatory decision-making processes can help ensure that the needs of marginalized communities and vulnerable groups are adequately addressed, promoting more resilient and equitable water and sanitation systems.

Technological advancements also hold extraordinary potential in enhancing water efficiency and resilience. Investments in research and development, alongside transfer of appropriate, cost-effective and environmentally sustainable technologies, can bolster water resource management. Solutions like smart water metering, water reclamation and reuse, as well as satellite-based monitoring can all contribute to more efficient and effective water systems.

International cooperation also plays a vital role in achieving SDG 6. Enhanced bilateral and multilateral partnerships can foster knowledge exchange, technical assistance, and innovative financing mechanisms. The provision of Official Development Assistance (ODA) targeted towards improving water and sanitation infrastructure, capacity development, and governance is crucial, particularly for low-income and vulnerable countries.
