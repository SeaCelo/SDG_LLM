India Agency for Partnership in Development
Analytical Report on SDG 2: Zero Hunger 

Introduction:
This report aims to provide an in-depth analysis of SDG 2: Zero Hunger and its current status in India. With a focus on addressing hunger, improving nutrition, and promoting sustainable agriculture, SDG 2 has been set to ensure food security, maintain sustainable food production systems, and eradicate all forms of malnutrition by 2030. Through this report, we aim to inform and guide strategic actions by presenting a comprehensive understanding of the current status of SDG 2 in India.

Overview of the Situation:
India's prime goal regarding SDG 2 is to eliminate hunger and achieve food security for its population of more than 1.3 billion people. Despite progress being made in areas such as poverty reduction, agricultural growth, and increasing food production, India continues to face significant challenges in eradicating hunger and malnutrition comprehensively.

Extent of Hunger and Malnutrition:
The latest data reveals that India is home to the largest number of hungry people globally, accounting for nearly one-third of the world's undernourished population. Despite a slight improvement in recent years, the prevalence of undernourishment remains unacceptably high, especially among marginalized populations and rural communities.

Factors Contributing to Hunger and Malnutrition:
Addressing the root causes of hunger requires a deep understanding of the multiple factors contributing to it. In India, issues such as poverty, lack of access to nutritious food, inadequate sanitation, gender inequalities, climate change impacts, and regional disparities exacerbate the challenge of achieving SDG 2. Additionally, conflicts and crises further impede efforts to eliminate hunger, particularly in conflict-affected regions.

Promoting Sustainable Agriculture:
Achieving food security and eliminating hunger necessitates a shift towards sustainable agricultural practices. India has been actively working towards this by promoting initiatives such as organic farming, precision agriculture, crop diversification, and sustainable water management. The implementation of these practices not only improves food production but also protects the environment, conserves natural resources, and assists in climate change mitigation.

Government Policies and Initiatives:
To enhance progress towards SDG 2, the Indian Government has introduced various policies and initiatives. The National Food Security Act ensures that food grains are available to vulnerable populations at affordable prices through the Public Distribution System. The National Nutrition Mission is aimed at improving maternal and child nutrition with a multi-sectoral approach. Similarly, numerous agricultural reforms have been introduced to enhance farmers' income and productivity.

Challenges and Way Forward:
Though efforts have been made, several key challenges persist in achieving SDG 2 in India. These include persistent poverty, slow agricultural growth, inadequate investment in the agricultural sector, climate change impacts, water scarcity, and limited access to quality healthcare, education, and social protection. Addressing these challenges necessitates multi-stakeholder collaborations, policy coherence, innovative interventions, and enhanced investments in research and development.

Conclusion:
SDG 2: Zero Hunger is critical for India's sustainable development. Eradicating hunger and achieving food security requires comprehensive and integrated strategies, focusing on addressing the diverse and interconnected factors.
