SDG 3: Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages

By Maurice Obstfeld, Ph.D. 
Professor of Economics, University of California, Berkeley

The third Sustainable Development Goal (SDG 3) represents an ambitious yet crucial endeavor to establish a comprehensive framework for global health and well-being. As an economist, I recognize the profound implications of this objective for economic development, productivity, and human capital accumulation. Addressing health challenges is not merely a moral imperative but also a catalyst for unlocking economic potential and fostering sustainable growth trajectories.

The multidimensional nature of SDG 3 necessitates a holistic approach that transcends traditional healthcare interventions. It demands a concerted effort to tackle the underlying determinants of health, including poverty, inadequate nutrition, lack of access to clean water and sanitation, and environmental factors. This goal underscores the intrinsic linkages between health, socioeconomic conditions, and environmental sustainability, aligning with the overarching principles of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development.

One of the critical targets within SDG 3 is the reduction of maternal mortality ratios and preventable deaths of newborns and children under five years of age. These objectives have far-reaching implications for human capital development and intergenerational well-being. Investing in maternal and child health not only improves immediate health outcomes but also lays the foundation for a healthier and more productive workforce in the long run.

Moreover, SDG 3 emphasizes the need to combat communicable diseases, such as HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, malaria, and hepatitis. These diseases disproportionately affect marginalized communities and impede economic progress, perpetuating cycles of poverty and inequality. Effective interventions, including improved access to diagnostics, treatments, and prevention measures, can mitigate the socioeconomic burdens associated with these diseases and promote inclusive growth.

Another critical aspect of SDG 3 is the recognition of non-communicable diseases (NCDs) as a growing global health challenge. NCDs, such as cardiovascular diseases, cancer, chronic respiratory diseases, and diabetes, are often linked to lifestyle factors, environmental exposures, and aging populations. Addressing NCDs requires a multifaceted approach that encompasses prevention, early detection, and effective management, as well as addressing the social determinants of health that contribute to their prevalence.

Importantly, SDG 3 acknowledges the need for universal health coverage (UHC) and access to quality essential healthcare services. UHC is not only a moral imperative but also a critical economic enabler, as it protects individuals and households from catastrophic health expenditures and promotes a healthier, more productive workforce. Achieving UHC requires robust health systems, adequate financing mechanisms, and a skilled healthcare workforce, which can be facilitated through international cooperation and knowledge-sharing.

Furthermore, SDG 3 recognizes the complex interplay between health, environmental factors, and sustainable development. Ensuring healthy lives necessitates addressing environmental challenges, such as air pollution, water contamination, and exposure to hazardous chemicals and waste. These environmental factors disproportionately impact vulnerable populations and can exacerbate existing health disparities, underscoring the need for integrated policies and interventions that promote sustainable development while safeguarding public health.

Achieving SDG 3 requires a concerted effort from various stakeholders, including governments, international organizations, civil society, and the private sector. Effective implementation necessitates robust financing mechanisms, capacity-building initiatives, and international cooperation to facilitate knowledge-sharing, technology transfer, and the mobilization of resources.

In conclusion, SDG 3 represents a transformative vision for global health and well-being, recognizing the intrinsic linkages between health, economic development, and environmental sustainability. By addressing the underlying determinants of health, promoting universal health coverage, and fostering international cooperation, we can unlock the full potential of human capital and pave the way for sustainable and inclusive economic growth. As an economist, I firmly believe that investing in health is not only a moral imperative but also a strategic investment in our collective future prosperity.
