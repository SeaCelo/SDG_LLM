Good health and well-being for all is a noble goal that demands unwavering commitment and concerted efforts from all stakeholders. As Swaminathan Aiyar, an eminent economist and columnist, I firmly believe that achieving SDG 3 is not just a matter of moral imperative but also a prerequisite for sustainable development and economic prosperity.

The inextricable link between health and economic growth has been well-established. A healthy population is a productive population, capable of contributing to the workforce, driving innovation, and fostering a vibrant economy. Conversely, poor health poses a significant burden on individuals, communities, and nations, leading to lost productivity, increased healthcare costs, and a vicious cycle of poverty and ill-health.

India's journey towards universal health coverage has been arduous, with persistent challenges such as inadequate healthcare infrastructure, shortage of skilled medical personnel, and a growing burden of non-communicable diseases. However, the country has made notable strides in recent years, with initiatives like Ayushman Bharat, which aims to provide affordable healthcare to millions of underprivileged citizens. Yet, much more needs to be done to ensure equitable access to quality healthcare services, particularly in rural and remote areas.

One of the key challenges in achieving SDG 3 is the vast disparity in healthcare accessibility and outcomes across different socioeconomic strata. While the affluent can afford high-end medical facilities, the marginalized sections of society often struggle to access even basic healthcare services. This inequity not only undermines the principles of social justice but also hinders the country's overall development trajectory.

To address this issue, a multifaceted approach is imperative. Firstly, we must prioritize investments in strengthening the public healthcare system, enhancing infrastructure, and ensuring adequate availability of trained medical professionals across all regions. Secondly, efforts must be made to promote preventive healthcare through awareness campaigns, encouraging healthy lifestyles, and addressing social determinants of health such as poverty, malnutrition, and lack of access to clean water and sanitation.

Furthermore, the private sector can play a pivotal role in complementing public healthcare initiatives. Through public-private partnerships and innovative financing models, we can leverage the expertise and resources of the private sector to enhance healthcare delivery, research, and innovation. Incentivizing private investment in healthcare infrastructure, particularly in underserved areas, can help bridge the accessibility gap.

However, achieving universal health coverage is not solely a matter of increasing healthcare spending or infrastructure development. It also requires a paradigm shift in our approach to healthcare, moving towards a more holistic and integrated model that embraces traditional and alternative systems of medicine. India's rich heritage of Ayurveda, Yoga, and other ancient healing practices holds immense potential in promoting preventive and sustainable healthcare practices.

Moreover, addressing the social determinants of health is crucial for achieving SDG 3. Factors such as poverty, inadequate nutrition, lack of education, and environmental degradation have a profound impact on individual and community health. Collaborative efforts across various sectors, including education, agriculture, water and sanitation, and environmental conservation, are essential to create an enabling environment for good health and well-being.
