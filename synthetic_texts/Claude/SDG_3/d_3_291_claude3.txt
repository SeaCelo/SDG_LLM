Achieving SDG 3: Ensuring Healthy Lives and Promoting Well-Being for All

By Tim Bollerslev, Ph.D.
Professor of Economics, Duke University

The Sustainable Development Goal 3 (SDG 3) – "Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages" – is a complex and multifaceted challenge that requires a holistic and integrated approach. As a leading expert in financial econometrics and risk management, I recognize the inextricable link between economic factors, public health, and sustainable development. Addressing SDG 3 necessitates a comprehensive understanding of the intricate interplay between socioeconomic determinants, healthcare systems, and the broader environmental and political landscapes.

At the core of SDG 3 lies the fundamental human right to healthcare and well-being. However, the stark reality is that access to quality healthcare remains a privilege for many, with profound disparities existing within and across nations. These inequalities are exacerbated by a myriad of factors, including poverty, inadequate infrastructure, lack of education, and sociocultural barriers. Achieving universal health coverage (UHC) is a critical imperative, but it requires addressing these underlying determinants through a coordinated and multisectoral approach.

One of the key challenges in realizing SDG 3 is the ongoing burden of communicable diseases, particularly in low- and middle-income countries. Diseases such as HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, malaria, and neglected tropical diseases continue to devastate communities, undermine economic productivity, and perpetuate cycles of poverty. Effective disease surveillance, prevention, and control strategies are essential, but they must be coupled with efforts to strengthen healthcare systems, improve access to essential medicines, and address the social determinants that fuel disease transmission.

Moreover, the global rise in non-communicable diseases (NCDs) – including cardiovascular diseases, cancers, chronic respiratory diseases, and diabetes – poses a formidable threat to sustainable development. NCDs are often linked to lifestyle factors, such as unhealthy diets, physical inactivity, and tobacco use, but they are also influenced by broader environmental and socioeconomic factors. Addressing NCDs requires a multifaceted approach that combines public health interventions, health promotion campaigns, and policies that create enabling environments for healthy living.

Another critical aspect of SDG 3 is maternal, newborn, and child health. Despite significant progress in reducing maternal and child mortality rates, preventable deaths and morbidities remain unacceptably high in many regions. Ensuring access to quality prenatal care, skilled birth attendants, and essential obstetric and neonatal care services is crucial, but it must be complemented by efforts to empower women, promote gender equality, and address the socioeconomic determinants that influence maternal and child health outcomes.

Furthermore, SDG 3 recognizes the importance of mental health and well-being, which have long been neglected in global health discourse. Mental health disorders, including depression, anxiety, and substance abuse, have profound impacts on individuals, families, and communities, and they are often exacerbated by social, economic, and environmental stressors. Addressing mental health requires a paradigm shift that destigmatizes mental illness, promotes mental health literacy, and integrates mental health services into primary healthcare systems.

Achieving SDG 3 also requires addressing the complex challenges posed by environmental health risks, such as air pollution, water contamination, and exposure to hazardous substances. These risks disproportionately impact vulnerable populations and have far-reaching consequences for human health, economic productivity, and sustainable development. Mitigating environmental health risks necessitates a collaborative effort involving multiple sectors, including public health, environmental protection, urban planning, and industry regulation.

Notably, the COVID-19 pandemic has underscored the critical importance of robust and resilient healthcare systems, as well as the need for effective global collaboration and preparedness for future health emergencies. The pandemic has exacerbated existing inequalities, disrupted essential health services, and highlighted the interconnectedness of human health, economic stability, and sustainable development.

In my role as an economist and risk management expert, I recognize the pivotal role played by financial resources and economic policies in achieving SDG 3. Adequate and sustainable financing for healthcare systems, public health interventions, and related initiatives is essential. However, it is equally important to address the broader economic determinants of health, such as poverty, income inequality, and access to education and employment opportunities.

Innovative financing mechanisms, such as impact investing, public-private partnerships, and risk-sharing arrangements, can help mobilize resources and promote sustainable healthcare financing. Additionally, economic policies that promote inclusive growth, reduce inequalities, and create enabling environments for healthy living are crucial for achieving SDG 3.

Furthermore, the integration of risk management principles and methodologies can enhance the effectiveness and sustainability of public health interventions. By incorporating risk assessment, risk monitoring, and risk mitigation strategies, we can better anticipate and respond to emerging health threats, manage uncertainties, and optimize resource allocation.

In conclusion, achieving SDG 3 – ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all – is a complex and multidimensional challenge that requires a holistic and integrated approach. It demands a sustained commitment from all stakeholders, including governments, civil society organizations, the private sector, and international bodies. By leveraging multisectoral collaborations, harnessing innovative financing mechanisms, and integrating risk management principles, we can create resilient and equitable healthcare systems, address the social and environmental determinants of health, and pave the way for sustainable development.
