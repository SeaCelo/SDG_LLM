While the region has witnessed a remarkable reduction in extreme poverty over the past decades, pockets of deprivation and vulnerability persist, exacerbated by factors such as climate change, conflicts, and economic shocks.

In this context, the Asian Development Bank (ADB) has played a pivotal role in supporting its developing member countries in their efforts to eradicate poverty in all its forms. Through a multidimensional approach that encompasses policy reforms, institutional strengthening, and targeted investments, the ADB has sought to address the root causes of poverty and promote inclusive growth.

One of the key strategies employed by the ADB has been the promotion of sustainable livelihoods and income-generating opportunities for the poor and vulnerable populations. This has involved supporting micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs), which are critical drivers of employment and income in many Asian economies. The ADB has provided technical assistance, access to finance, and capacity-building programs to enhance the competitiveness and resilience of these enterprises.

Furthermore, the ADB has placed a strong emphasis on enhancing access to basic services, such as education, healthcare, clean water, and sanitation, which are essential for breaking the intergenerational cycle of poverty. Investments in these areas have not only improved the living conditions of the poor but also contributed to building human capital and fostering economic productivity.

Recognizing the disproportionate impact of poverty on women and marginalized groups, the ADB has mainstreamed gender equality and social inclusion considerations across its operations. This has involved supporting legal and policy reforms, promoting women's economic empowerment, and addressing cultural and institutional barriers that perpetuate discrimination and exclusion.

Importantly, the ADB has acknowledged the inextricable link between poverty and environmental degradation, and has prioritized sustainable natural resource management and climate change adaptation and mitigation measures. This has included promoting renewable energy sources, sustainable agriculture practices, and disaster risk management strategies, among others.

To enhance the effectiveness of its interventions, the ADB has fostered partnerships with governments, civil society organizations, and the private sector, recognizing the need for a coordinated and collaborative approach to addressing the complex challenges of poverty.

However, despite these efforts, significant challenges persist. The COVID-19 pandemic has reversed decades of progress in poverty reduction, with millions pushed back into poverty due to lost livelihoods and disrupted services. Moreover, the impacts of climate change, particularly on the region's most vulnerable populations, threaten to undermine hard-won gains.

As such, the ADB's approach to SDG 1 must evolve to address these emerging challenges. This may involve exploring innovative financing mechanisms, leveraging digital technologies to enhance service delivery, and promoting resilient and inclusive development pathways that prioritize the needs of the most marginalized.

Ultimately, the path towards achieving SDG 1 in Asia will require sustained political commitment, robust governance frameworks, and a concerted effort to address the underlying structural inequalities and vulnerabilities that perpetuate poverty. The ADB, through its strategic interventions, technical expertise, and convening power, remains well-positioned to support its developing member countries in this critical endeavor.
