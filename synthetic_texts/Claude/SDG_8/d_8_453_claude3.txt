WaterAid's Report on Sustainable Development Goal 8: Decent Work and Economic Growth

The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development recognizes the pivotal role of decent work and economic growth in driving progress across multiple fronts. SDG 8 not only seeks to promote inclusive, sustainable economic growth and productive employment, but also highlights the interdependence between water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) access and decent working conditions.

Water scarcity, inadequate sanitation, and poor hygiene practices profoundly impact workplace productivity, employee health and absenteeism rates. In regions grappling with WASH challenges, such as sub-Saharan Africa and parts of Asia, the economic toll is staggering. It is estimated that a lack of access to safe water and sanitation costs the global economy over $260 billion annually due to lost productivity.

Conversely, investments in WASH interventions can yield substantial economic returns. For instance, a hypothetical initiative by WaterAid in rural Bangladesh aimed to improve WASH facilities in garment factories. By providing safe drinking water, adequate sanitation, and hygiene education, absenteeism rates among female workers dropped by 25%, directly boosting productivity and economic output. Additionally, the reduced incidence of waterborne diseases among employees resulted in healthcare cost savings for both workers and employers.

The nexus between WASH and decent work extends beyond formal employment settings. In many developing countries, a significant portion of the workforce is engaged in informal or entrepreneurial activities, such as street vending, agriculture, or home-based enterprises. Lack of access to safe water and sanitation disproportionately impacts these vulnerable populations, hindering their economic prospects and trapping them in cycles of poverty.

Imagine a fictional scenario where WaterAid partners with local authorities in a peri-urban settlement in Kenya to establish community-managed water kiosks and public sanitation facilities. This intervention not only improves WASH access for residents but also enables the establishment of new income-generating opportunities. Women, previously burdened by the arduous task of fetching water from distant sources, can now allocate their time to productive economic activities, such as starting small businesses or engaging in skilled labor.

A hypothetical initiative by WaterAid in collaboration with the private sector could involve improving WASH infrastructure and promoting good hygiene practices in rural communities supplying labor to commercial agricultural operations. Such interventions not only safeguard worker health and well-being but also enhance the sustainability and competitiveness of these industries in the global market.

Achieving SDG 8 requires a multi-stakeholder approach, with governments, civil society organizations, and the private sector working in concert. Policymakers must create an enabling environment that promotes inclusive economic growth, invests in WASH infrastructure, and protects worker rights. Businesses, on the other hand, can contribute by adopting sustainable practices, providing decent working conditions, and supporting WASH initiatives in their operational areas and supply chains.

In conclusion, the interdependencies between SDG 8 and WASH access are profound and far-reaching. By addressing WASH challenges, we can unlock economic opportunities, enhance workplace productivity, and drive sustainable, inclusive growth. Conversely, progress toward decent work and economic development can catalyze improvements in WASH access, creating a virtuous cycle that advances multiple Sustainable Development Goals simultaneously.
